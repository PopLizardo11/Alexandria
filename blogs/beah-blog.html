A Look into Sociological Dynamics: Exploitation and Rebellion in Frankenstein

Written by Jake Petrasanta - Dec. 17, 2024

Frankenstein, the novel by Mary Shelley, is an insightful review of the economic, social, and intellectual forces that dominated the Industrial Revolution. The novel examines the damaging effects of class strife, alienation, and exploitation through the prism of Marxist ideology. Marxist criticism, according to Cole and Oglesby (2023), links social and political problems to disputes between members of various socioeconomic groups. Shelley emphasizes the dehumanizing consequences of industrialization and the bourgeoisie's contempt for the working class by focusing on Victor Frankenstein's encounter with the beast. Originating from the aspirations of the scientific bourgeoisie, the creature represents the downtrodden proletariat, which is alienated, exploited, and consequently driven to rebellion.

The bourgeois class, represented by Victor Frankenstein, uses ambition, power, and the desire for self-interest to establish control over social systems (Ryan, 2024). His scientific pursuits, like those discussed in Chapter 4, are a reflection of capitalism's ambition to control nature and exploit resources. According to Marxist theory, Victor is a capitalist who employs scientific instruments and experiments to create something new while ignoring or avoiding accountability for the results of his labor.

Furthermore, Victor’s obsession with managing life and death, particularly obvious in Chapter 5 as he animates the creature, metaphorically represents the capitalist pursuit of unrestrained production without concern for ethical or social consequences. Victor prioritizes the process of creation over the well-being of the final product, much like an industrial-era factory owner would. In the same way as capitalists frequently ignore the laborers who produce their wealth, he recoils in disgust after finishing his work and leaves the creature behind.

Victor's scientific aspirations gave rise to the creature, which represents the oppressed underclass, or proletariat (Butler, 2024). Both Victor and society at large reject the creature, which is first characterized in Chapters 10–12 as innocent and yearning for company. This rejection reflects the alienation that the working class experiences when they are dismissed as nothing more than industrial equipment.

The creature's increasing awareness of human culture highlights its estrangement in Chapter 15, when it comes across Paradise Lost, Plutarch's Lives, and The Sorrows of Werter. Its awareness of its marginalized status is heightened by these books, which intensifies its animosity for Victor. Chapters 16–20 describe the creature's growing resentment and ultimately actions of retaliation, which can be seen as the proletariat's inevitable revolt against the bourgeoisie as a result of years of institutionalized tyranny.

Alienation is a major Marxist motif in Frankenstein, and it shows up for both Victor and the monster. Marx's theory of alienation holds that under capitalism, workers become separated from their labor, the goods they produce, and themselves (Leopold, 2022). As demonstrated in Chapters 3-5, Victor feels alienated from his loved ones as a result of his compulsive quest for knowledge.

The alienation is even deeper for the creature. Similar to the proletariat's alienation from the fruits of their labor, the creature describes its early experiences of desertion. It is denied the company it longs for, shunned by the human race, and viewed as a monster due to its appearance. The creature's request for a mate, an equal to share its misery, is denied by Victor in Chapter 16, signifying the capitalist's reluctance to provide the downtrodden with solidarity or collective action, further solidifies this alienation (Erol, n.d.).

The dehumanizing consequences of industrialization are reflected in Victor's act of creating life through scientific experimentation. His unrelenting obsession with dissecting corpses and tampering with nature in Chapter 4 is a prime example of how the Industrial Revolution mechanized labor. The creature is viewed as an object of Victor's ambition rather than a sentient human, much like industrial workers are reduced to gears in a machine.

The instant terror Victor has upon witnessing the creature serves as a critique of this mechanization, highlighting the emotional and moral vacuum created by industrial advancement. According to Thachduryany (2017), Shelley criticizes how industrialization eliminates humanity from the creative process and turns both workers and goods into commodities.

The proletariat's revolt against the bourgeoisie can be seen as symbolized by the creature's deeds of vengeance, especially the killing of William in Chapter 16. According to Marxist theory, the working class will rise up and overturn the capitalist system after being exploited for a long time. In a similar vein, the creature's behavior results from systematic tyranny and neglect rather than from inherent malice.

The bourgeois fear of oppressed people standing together is emphasized by Victor's refusal to give the creature a female partner. Although Victor crushes the creature's dream for company out of concern that a new "race" may threaten his authority, Marxist philosophy emphasizes that solidarity among the working class is necessary for revolution.

The self-destructive inclinations of capitalism are symbolized by Victor's unrelenting search for the monster across the Arctic. The bourgeoisie's fruitless attempts to quell the proletariat's uprising are reflected in his fixation with destroying his creation. The unviability of a society based on exploitation and contempt for human needs is highlighted by Victor's final demise.

In a similar vein, the creature's choice to terminate its existence at the book's end represents the existential hopelessness resulting from estrangement. As a symbol of the breakdown of a capitalist system that is unable to satisfy the needs of the working class, it acknowledges that its misery cannot be reconciled within the current social framework (Kenton, 2024).

Frankenstein's portrayal of nature contrasts sharply with the dehumanizing consequences of industrialization. Victor and the creature find momentary comfort in the Alps' breathtaking grandeur, which represents a world free from capitalist exploitation. This link to nature raises questions about the artificiality of industrial capitalism and its propensity to rob real human experiences.

For the creature, moments of reflection in nature, as seen in Chapter 12, offer temporary reprieve from its alienation. These scenes suggest that a return to natural harmony, rather than relentless industrial progress, may offer a path toward genuine connection and fulfillment.

By exposing the damaging effects of class conflict, alienation, and exploitation, a Marxist interpretation of Frankenstein reveals the book as a biting indictment of capitalism. While the creature's revolt represents the inevitable uprising of the downtrodden, Victor Frankenstein's treatment of the creature is comparable to the bourgeoisie's exploitation of the working class. Mary Shelley offers a timeless cautionary tale about the repercussions of a system that puts production and power ahead of people by exposing the shortcomings of industrialization and the dehumanizing impacts of unbridled ambition. Shelley urges a restoration to human connection and community responsibility by reevaluating society ideals through the interaction of creation and destruction.
